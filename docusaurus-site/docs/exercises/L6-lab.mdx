## Problem 4.2

Consider the clause set

$$
\neg p(z,a) \lor \neg p(z,x) \lor \neg p(x,z),\qquad
p(y,a) \lor p(y,f(y)),\qquad
p(w,a) \lor p(f(w),w).
$$

Give a refutation using the non-ground binary resolution calculus $\mathbb{BR}$. For every derived clause, state its parents, the inference rule, and the most general unifier.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Let $(1)$–$(3)$ denote the input clauses.

1. Negative factoring of $(1)$ with $\{x \mapsto a\}$ yields $(4)\; \neg p(z,a) \lor \neg p(a,z)$.
2. Negative factoring of $(4)$ with $\{z \mapsto a\}$ gives $(5)\; \neg p(a,a)$.
3. Resolving $(5)$ with $(2)$ using $\{y \mapsto a\}$ produces $(6)\; p(a,f(a))$.
4. Resolving $(4)$ with $(6)$ using $\{z \mapsto f(a)\}$ produces $(7)\; \neg p(f(a),a)$.
5. Resolving $(3)$ with $(7)$ using $\{w \mapsto a\}$ yields $(8)\; p(a,a)$.
6. Resolving $(5)$ and $(8)$ yields the empty clause $\square$.

</details>


## Problem 4.3

Let $p$ be a unary predicate, $f$ a unary function, $x,y$ variables, and $c$ a constant. For the clauses $C_1 = p(x) \lor p(y)$, $C_2 = p(x)$, and $D = p(f(c))$, determine which subsume $D$.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

1. $C_1$ does not subsume $D$: every instance $C_1\theta$ has two literals and therefore cannot be a sub-multiset of $D$.
2. $C_2$ subsumes $D$: with $\theta = \{x \mapsto f(c)\}$, $C_2\theta$ matches $D$.

</details>


## Problem 4.4

Provide a superposition refutation (without binary resolution) of $\{x = f(c),\ a \neq b\}$ such that no inference combines symbols from $\{f,c\}$ with $\{a,b\}$.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

1. $f(c) = x$ (axiom)
2. $a \neq b$ (axiom)
3. Superposition of two renamed copies of (1) gives $x = y$.
4. Superposition of (2) with (3) yields $a \neq x$.
5. Equality resolution on $a \neq x$ with $x \mapsto a$ produces $\square$.

</details>


## Problem 4.5

Let $f$ be unary, $g$ binary, weights $w(s) = 1$ for all symbols, and precedence $g \gg f \gg c \gg b \gg a$. Decide the following redundancy questions under the induced KBO:

1. Do $C_1: a \neq b \lor f(a) \neq a$ and $C_2: f(f(x)) = a$ make $C_3: f(f(b)) \neq b \lor f(a) \neq a$ redundant?
2. Does $C_4: f(g(x,a)) \neq f(y)$ make $C_5: f(g(x,z)) \neq f(g(y,b)) \lor f(g(x,b)) \neq f(g(a,b))$ redundant?
3. Does $C_6: g(x,y) \neq f(x)$ make $C_7: g(f(x),f(z)) \neq f(f(x)) \lor g(a,b) \neq c$ redundant?

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

1. No. $f(f(x)) = a$ and the disjuncts in $C_3$ are incomparable under KBO, so $C_3$ is not redundant.
2. Yes. $C_4$ is strictly smaller and already inconsistent, rendering $C_5$ redundant.
3. Yes. Renaming $z$ to $y$ in $C_7$, the literal $g(f(x),f(y)) \neq f(f(x))$ instantiates $g(x,y) \neq f(x)$, and $C_6$ is smaller, so $C_7$ is redundant.

</details>


## Problem 4.6

Examine the superposition inference

$$
\frac{x = f(c) \lor p(x)\qquad f(h(b)) = h(g(y,y)) \lor h(g(d,b)) \neq f(c)}{p(h(g(d,b))) \lor f(h(b)) = h(g(y,y))}
$$

in $\mathbb{SUP}$ (no binary resolution). Is it sound? Is it simplifying?

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Soundness: Any model satisfying both premises either has $f(h(b)) = h(g(y,y))$, in which case the conclusion holds, or $h(g(d,b)) \neq f(c)$, forcing $p(h(g(d,b)))$ via the first premise. Thus the conclusion is also satisfied.

Simplifying: Let $C_1$, $C_2$ denote the premises and $D$ the conclusion. Counter-models show neither $C_2, D \models C_1$ nor $C_1, D \models C_2$, so the inference is not simplifying.

</details>


## Problem 4.7

Show that the inverse of a dense order is dense. Outline a TPTP formalisation and the key steps in Vampire’s refutation (`-av off`).

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Encode strict total order axioms and density for $r_1$, plus clauses asserting $r_2(y,x)$ iff $r_1(x,y)$. Negate density for $r_2$ and use Vampire to derive $\square$. The proof relies on superposing the inverse-axiom clauses with the negated density clause; documenting the critical steps (premises, MGUs, conclusions) shows how the contradiction arises.

</details>


## Problem 4.8

Using the group axioms (associativity, left identity, inverses), prove that the left identity $e$ is also a right identity via TPTP + Vampire (`-av off`). Summarise the superposition steps that lead to the contradiction.

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Model the axioms and negate $x \cdot e = x$. Vampire’s refutation chains superposition steps: e.g. combining the inverse axiom with the negated goal to derive clauses that ultimately contradict associativity/identity axioms. Listing the crucial inferences (with MGUs) highlights how the contradiction—and hence the right-identity property—emerges.

</details>
